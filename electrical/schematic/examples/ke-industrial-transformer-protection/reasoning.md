# Reasoning — KE Industrial Transformer Protection (IDMT 51 + REF 87N)

## Why IDMT + REF (and not full 87T differential) for 1000 kVA

Transformer differential (87T) is the gold-standard primary protection for power transformers, but it has a cost-vs-benefit threshold that sits around 2.5-5 MVA. Below that threshold, the saved insurance value of an 87T scheme is small relative to the additional CT cost (matched HV + LV CT pairs at higher classes), interposing-CT cost (or numerical phase-and-ratio compensation in the relay), and engineering / commissioning time. For a 1000 kVA Dyn11 distribution transformer in a Kenyan industrial workshop, the canonical protection scheme is IDMT phase overcurrent (51) on the HV side, residual earth fault (51N) using the same phase CTs, and high-impedance restricted earth fault (87N) on the LV neutral. This package costs roughly 20-30% of an 87T installation and provides ~95% of the protection coverage. The remaining 5% gap (turn-to-turn faults on the same phase, which 87T detects but 51 does not) is accepted at the 1000 kVA tier as a known limitation.

ABB's REF615 and Siemens' 7SR23 product families both bundle 51 + 51N + 87N into a single multifunction IED at this price point, which is the reason the schematic shows R51 and R87N as physically separate items on paper but expects them to be the same physical device in the panel. The schematic-zone separation is preserved on the drawing because the protection ZONES (phase-fault zone vs restricted-earth zone) are conceptually distinct even when the implementing device is identical — and the drafting discipline of separating them on the schematic helps the next engineer trace which CTs feed which function.

## R51 pickup at 100 A primary (1.9× HV FLA)

1000 kVA at 11 kV = 52.5 A HV full-load current. CT primary at 100 A gives a pickup-to-FLA ratio of 1.9. This sits in the IEC 60255-151 sweet spot of 1.5×-2.5× — enough margin to ride through cold-load pickup transients (cold-load inrush on a transformer that has been off for hours can briefly hit 1.5× FLA as the magnetising current temporarily increases), but well below the minimum fault current. The minimum fault current at the HV terminals during a downstream LV three-phase fault reflects back through the Dyn11 vector as roughly 2 kA at 11 kV (using transformer impedance 5%), which is 20× the relay pickup — comfortable grading margin.

The IEC SI (Standard Inverse) curve at TMS 0.2 gives approximately 0.4 s operate time at 6 kA — this is the typical fault level the relay would actually see on a downstream three-phase LV fault (reflected through the transformer). 0.4 s coordinates above the ~250 ms total clearing time of a typical LV MCCB on the 415 V side, which preserves selectivity: a downstream LV fault is cleared by the LV MCCB, not by the HV CB.

## R87N high-impedance scheme — 5% pickup, 470 ohm stabilising resistor

High-impedance differential (also called the "high-Z" or "Merz-Price" scheme) is the canonical implementation for restricted earth fault on distribution transformers because it gives instantaneous operation (no time delay), high sensitivity (typically 5-20% pickup), and intrinsic stability against external faults via the stabilising resistor mechanism. The scheme principle: under healthy conditions or external faults, the four CTs (three phase + one neutral) produce balanced currents that sum to zero at the relay; under an internal fault inside the zone (HV winding to earth, for example), the neutral CT carries unbalanced current that develops a voltage across the high-impedance relay coil + stabilising resistor exceeding the relay setting.

The stabilising resistor value (Rs = 470 ohm in this design) is sized using the standard formula Vs = If × (Rct + 2 × Rl), where Vs is the stabilising voltage above which the scheme operates, If is the maximum through-fault current at the CT secondary, Rct is the CT winding resistance, and Rl is the lead resistance to the relay. For typical CT class PX with Rct = 2 ohm, lead resistance Rl = 1 ohm per leg, and through-fault CT secondary current of 100 A (= 2000 A primary through-fault, ÷ 20 CT ratio), Vs = 100 × (2 + 2) = 400 V. The stabilising resistor is sized so the developed voltage across it under maximum through-fault stays at ~80% of the relay knee-point voltage — 470 ohm is the standard catalogue value that achieves this for a 5 A CT secondary with a typical 220 V relay knee-point.

CT class PX (formerly Class X) is mandatory for high-impedance schemes because the scheme stability depends on linear CT response under through-fault conditions. A class 5P10 CT (which is fine for IDMT phase protection) would saturate non-linearly above its knee-point and produce false residual current at the relay, compromising the scheme stability. T1N is therefore specified as class PX, distinct from T1A/T1B/T1C which are class 5P10.

## KE jurisdiction routing

KS 1700:2018 is the Kenyan adoption of the IET Wiring Regulations (BS 7671). Annex E §VIII establishes the explicit mechanism by which BS 7671:2018+A2:2022 clauses are adopted verbatim into the Kenyan standard. The schematic accordingly uses the form `"KS 1700:2018 § X.Y.Z (Annex E §VIII: adopts BS 7671:2018+A2:2022 § X.Y.Z verbatim)"` per Sprint 3-W2b citation discipline. Protection-relay standards (IEC 60255-151, IEC 60255-187), instrument-transformer standards (IEC 60044-1 / IEC 61869-2), and symbol standards (BS EN 60617 IEC 617-7) are cited directly because they are jurisdiction-agnostic and not transposed by any Kenyan national standard.

A note on IEC 60044-1 vs IEC 61869-2: IEC 61869-2:2012 superseded IEC 60044-1:2003 for CT performance specifications. However, IEC 60044-1 remains the standard most commonly cited on existing KE / UK distribution projects and on the datasheets of installed ABB / Siemens / Schneider CTs, because the manufacturer catalogues did not all migrate immediately to the IEC 61869 nomenclature. The schematic cites IEC 60044-1 as the primary reference with an explicit note in compliance_summary.assumptions[] acknowledging the supersession — this is the pragmatic choice for a 2026 design that needs to interoperate with field-installed equipment.

## fault-level + earthing intent consumption

Two intents are present and consumed. fault-level supplied `ifault_ka = 12 kA` at the 11 kV HV terminals, which is used in the assumptions[] to confirm the IDMT 51 pickup margin (12 kA / 100 A primary = 120× pickup, well above IEC 60255-151 § 4.4 minimum of 2× and well below CT saturation at 10× = 1000 A primary). earthing supplied the LV system declaration as TN-S with the transformer star-point separately earthed via a 0.4 ohm NER — this 0.4 ohm NER limits LV earth-fault current to ~600 A, which sits well above the R87N pickup window of 5% × 100 A primary residual = 5 A primary. db-layout-rollup is not present on this schematic and that absence is correct: the HV transformer-zone protection sits upstream of any LV distribution board, so there is no LV cascade to consume. The schematic is therefore in partial-hybrid mode (two of three potential intents consumed), with the absence of db-layout-rollup justified rather than flagged.
