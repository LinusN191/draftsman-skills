# Reasoning — UK Commercial 11kV Differential Transformer Protection (87T + 51 backup)

## Why 87T (and not just IDMT) at 2.5 MVA

The cost-vs-coverage threshold for transformer differential sits in the 1.5-3 MVA band for distribution transformers. Below that, the value of 87T's unique detection capability (turn-to-turn faults on the same phase, which IDMT cannot see because the through-current does not appreciably change) is small relative to the extra CT + relay cost. At 2.5 MVA, the calculus reverses: this is a commercial intake transformer with potentially hundreds of tenant supplies behind it, and a single failed turn-to-turn fault that escapes IDMT detection can escalate to a destructive winding burn-through over minutes. 87T catches that within milliseconds. The standard UK commercial-intake protection package at 2.5 MVA is therefore 87T primary + IDMT 51 backup on the HV side (this schematic) plus IDMT 51/51N + REF 87N on the LV side (a sibling schematic, not drawn here).

## R87T biased differential — 30% slope, 20% In pickup

The biased differential principle: the relay measures (I_HV - I_LV) where both currents are normalised through the relay's numerical phase + ratio compensation. Under healthy conditions or external faults, I_HV equals I_LV and the differential current is zero (modulo CT errors and ratio-compensation residual). Under an internal fault, I_HV does not equal I_LV (because some of the fault current bypasses the secondary side) and the differential current jumps. The "bias slope" is the percentage of through-current that the differential current must exceed before the relay operates — it stabilises the scheme against CT mismatch errors that scale with through-current. A 30% slope tolerates ~5% steady-state CT-error mismatch comfortably, with margin against momentary CT saturation during external faults.

The 20% In pickup is the minimum differential current the relay will respond to at zero through-current — set high enough to avoid sensitivity to CT no-load magnetising currents and low enough to detect light internal faults. 20% is the standard ABB / Siemens / Schneider recommended starting point per the manufacturer commissioning manuals (ABB 1MRK 504 116-UEN, Siemens 7UT63 manual, Schneider P64x application notes), and is universally accepted on UK DNO-approved protection settings.

Second-harmonic blocking (15% I2/I1 threshold) is mandatory: transformer magnetising inrush is rich in second harmonic and can otherwise trigger a false 87T trip on transformer energisation. Fifth-harmonic blocking (35% I5/I1) prevents false trips during over-excitation events (HV voltage briefly exceeding 110% of rated, which drives the core into saturation and produces a fifth-harmonic-rich exciting current).

## CT class 5P20 for both HV and LV

Class 5P20 means: 5% composite error at 20× rated primary current. The "20" in 5P20 is the accuracy-limit factor — the multiple of rated current at which the CT begins to saturate. For HV CTs at 200/5 ratio, 5P20 holds linearity up to 200 × 20 = 4000 A primary. The consumed fault-level intent gives 18 kA at the HV side — but the differential scheme doesn't see the full 18 kA in the secondary because the fault is upstream of the LV CTs (so only HV CTs energise, and they see the 18 kA HV fault as 18000/200 × 5 = 450 A secondary, well below saturation). For LV CTs at 4000/5 ratio, 5P20 holds linearity up to 4000 × 20 = 80 kA primary, well above the 35 kA LV fault level. CT saturation is the dominant failure mode of differential schemes (saturated CT produces unbalanced secondary current → false differential current → false trip), so the 5P20 + bias-slope combination is the standard safety margin.

## R51-HVBU backup at 1.2× FLA, VI curve TMS 0.3

The backup IDMT exists for two failure modes the 87T cannot cover: (a) 87T relay hardware failure (87T self-supervises but cannot fully self-test), and (b) DC trip-supply loss on the 87T circuit (the relay can be energised but the trip output cannot operate Q1). 51 backup wired to the same Q1 trip coil but with independent CT secondaries (paralleled at the X1 terminal block, NOT in series, because parallel CTs share the burden whilst series CTs would double-count the burden) provides redundancy against both modes.

Pickup at 240 A primary = 1.2× HV FLA (131 A) gives a tight margin: the relay does not pick up on legitimate overload or cold-load pickup transients but will detect any sustained current above 1.2× FLA. VI (Very Inverse) curve at TMS 0.3 is the standard UK DNO-grading curve for transformer backup — gives ~1.2 s at 6 kA fault, which grades above 87T (instantaneous at 100 ms) and below the upstream DNO 11 kV ring-main relay (typically ~2 s at 6 kA per ENA G99 typical settings). This 0.4 s grading interval between R51-HVBU and the upstream ring-main relay matches the BS 7671 § 314 discrimination grouping requirement.

## Two-lockout architecture: K86-HV + K86-LV

A single-lockout architecture (one K86 trips both Q1-HV and Q1-LV) saves a relay and some wiring but has a fatal-flaw failure mode: if the lockout coil itself fails (open-circuit coil) or the DC supply to the lockout drops out, the trip doesn't propagate and the transformer remains energised from both ends. Two lockouts give independent failure paths: K86-HV trips Q1-HV from the HV-side DC supply; K86-LV trips Q1-LV from the LV-side DC supply. The 87T trip output drives BOTH lockouts in parallel via independent contacts, so a single 87T trip command propagates to both ends even if one lockout fails. This is the BS 7671 § 537.2 isolation discipline expressed in protection terms.

## ENA G99 grid code reference

ENA Engineering Recommendation G99 Issue 1 is the UK Distribution Network Operator (DNO) interface framework. It primarily governs embedded-generation interface requirements (synchronous and inverter generators connecting to the DNO network), but G99 is also the framework reference for HV-side relay settings approval by the DNO on consumer intakes. UK DNOs (UKPN, SPEN, NPg, ENWL, WPD, SSEN) require formal review and approval of all HV relay settings before energisation — even on a passive consumer-only intake transformer like this 2.5 MVA. G99 is cited here as the framework reference for the R51-HVBU settings even though the transformer itself is not generation-coupled. The actual settings would be submitted to the DNO via the G99 application process and would carry the DNO's stamp before commissioning.

## fault-level + db-layout-rollup intent consumption

Two intents are present and consumed. fault-level supplied ifault_ka_HV = 18 kA and ifault_ka_LV = 35 kA — both values cross-check against the CT class 5P20 saturation knee and against the bias-slope 30% selection (which tolerates through-fault CT-error currents up to ~5% mismatch, well above the worst-case ratio-error contribution from the 200:4000 transformer ratio normalisation in the relay firmware). db-layout-rollup supplied main_switch_rating_a = 4000 A on the LV side, which matches the LV CT primary 4000/5 — confirming no mismatch between protection sensor (CT secondary) and load-current sensor (the ACB internal current sensor on Q1-LV). The earthing intent is not present in this consumed_intents list because the earthing single-line is a separate schematic and not directly referenced by the 87T scheme (the LV earthing system affects the 87N REF scheme on a sibling schematic, not the 87T differential).
