# NFPA 70E:2024 — Terminology and Glossary

## Arc-Rated (AR)
Clothing or equipment labeled and tested to withstand the thermal energy of an arc flash without igniting or shrinking excessively. Arc-rated equipment is assigned a rating in cal/cm² (calorie per square centimeter), also called the Arc Thermal Performance Value (ATPV). Workers must wear AR clothing matching or exceeding the predicted incident energy at the work location.

## Arc-Flash Boundary (AFB)
The distance from energized electrical equipment within which a worker could suffer a curable second-degree burn (1.2 cal/cm²) if an arc flash occurred. The AFB is defined by either a calculation (IEEE 1584 or NFPA 70E Annex D method) or the equipment table method in NFPA 70E §130.5(G). All workers within the AFB must wear arc-rated PPE appropriate to the incident energy.

## Incident Energy (E)
The thermal energy (in calories per square centimeter, cal/cm²) incident on a worker's skin at a specified distance from an arcing fault. Incident energy depends on the available short-circuit current, the clearing time of the protecting device, and the distance from the arc source. Higher incident energy requires higher PPE categories. Incident energy is either calculated using IEEE 1584 methods or determined using the NFPA 70E equipment table method per §130.5(G).

## Shock Approach Boundary — Limited Approach Boundary (LAB)
The distance from energized electrical parts inside which a shock hazard exists for unqualified persons. Within the LAB, unqualified persons must not approach without a safety escort. For AC systems, the LAB typically extends 10 feet (3.05 m) from energized parts; for DC systems, it is 3 feet (0.9 m). Crossing the LAB without proper training, PPE, and work authorization is prohibited.

## Shock Approach Boundary — Restricted Approach Boundary (RAB)
The nearer boundary within which shock hazard is severe and only qualified persons with specialized protective equipment may work. The RAB is closer than the LAB and requires additional precautions: shock-protective devices, insulating tools, or deenergizing. For AC systems, the RAB typically extends 3-4 feet (0.9–1.2 m) depending on voltage; for DC systems, it is 1 foot (0.3 m).

## PPE Category 1
The minimum arc-rated PPE level, required for incident energy ≤ 2 cal/cm². Typically includes FR shirt and pants (minimum ATPV 2 cal/cm²), hard hat, safety glasses, and hearing protection. Category 1 is used for routine switchgear operation and low-hazard tasks in equipment rated ≤ 1000 V.

## PPE Category 2
Arc-rated PPE for incident energy between > 2 and ≤ 4 cal/cm². Typically includes FR shirt, FR pants, arc-rated face shield or balaclava (ATPV ≥ 4 cal/cm²), hard hat, safety glasses, and hearing protection. Category 2 is common for many industrial distribution panels and routine maintenance on medium-voltage equipment.

## PPE Category 3
Arc-rated PPE for incident energy between > 4 and ≤ 8 cal/cm². Typically includes two-layer FR clothing system (total ATPV ≥ 8 cal/cm²), arc-rated hood (ATPV ≥ 8 cal/cm²), hard hat with face shield, safety glasses, and hearing protection. Category 3 is required for high-energy equipment, large transformer banks, and utility-scale distribution switchgear.

## PPE Category 4
The highest arc-rated PPE level, required for incident energy > 8 cal/cm² (up to 40 cal/cm²). Typically includes multi-layer, full-body AR coverage (ATPV ≥ 40 cal/cm²), arc-rated hood (ATPV ≥ 40 cal/cm²), hard hat with face shield, gloves, and foot protection. Category 4 is mandated for high-voltage substations, large utility switchgear, and arc-flash sources exceeding 40 cal/cm².

## Qualified Person
An individual who has training and demonstrated knowledge of electrical systems, hazard identification, and arc-flash risk. A qualified person is authorized to work on or near energized electrical equipment and must be competent to recognize hazards and take precautions to mitigate them. Per NFPA 70E §110.26(A), qualifications include knowledge of construction and operation of electrical equipment, understanding of hazards associated with electricity, and training in first aid and CPR.

## Energized Work Permit (EWP)
A documented authorization required when energized work is planned and the work cannot be performed with the equipment deenergized. The EWP must include the date, time, location, equipment involved, hazard analysis, required PPE and tools, rescue procedures, and approval by a qualified supervisor. The EWP is retained on file per §130.5(F).

## Risk Assessment
The systematic process of identifying electrical hazards, evaluating the likelihood and severity of potential incidents, and determining control measures (PPE, boundaries, work permits). NFPA 70E §130.5 mandates a documented risk assessment before any energized work. The assessment must evaluate shock risk (via §130.4 boundaries) and arc-flash risk (via §130.5 incident-energy or table method), resulting in specified PPE and work controls.

## Likelihood
The probability that a task could result in an arc-flash incident at a given location. NFPA 70E §130.5(C) provides a likelihood table indicating whether an arc-flash hazard is "likely" (probable) or "possible" (lower probability) for specified task categories and equipment types. Likelihood informs whether full incident-energy analysis is required or whether the table method (§130.5(G)) is acceptable.

## Hazard Analysis
The detailed evaluation of electrical hazards present at a work location, including identification of potential shock sources, arc-flash sources, and the sequence of events that could lead to injury. A hazard analysis is the foundation of the risk assessment process and drives the selection of control measures and PPE. NFPA 70E requires hazard analysis before energized work per §130.5(B).

## Electrical Safety Program
A documented, employer-established framework mandated by NFPA 70E §110.20 that includes policies, procedures, and training to ensure safe work with electrical systems. The electrical safety program must address lockout/tagout, energized work authorization, competency and training, hazard analysis, shock boundaries, arc-flash risk assessment, PPE, tools and test equipment, and incident investigation and reporting.

## Lockout/Tagout (LOTO)
The procedure of removing or isolating power from electrical equipment (lockout) and affixing a clearly visible tag (tagout) to prevent unexpected re-energization. LOTO is mandated for maintenance and repair work per NFPA 70E §130.2(A) and is the preferred control to eliminate arc-flash and shock hazards. Energized work is only performed when deenergizing would create an additional hazard per §130.2(A)(1).

## Arc-Resistant Equipment
Electrical equipment (typically switchgear or distribution boards) designed and tested to limit the release of arc plasma and pressure during an internal arc-flash event, thereby reducing injury risk to workers nearby. Arc-resistant enclosures are rated per IEEE/NFPA standards and may be specified where arc-flash incident energies exceed the practical PPE ratings.
