# NFPA 70E Annex H — PPE Selection Guidance

NFPA 70E:2024 Annex H provides reference guidance for selecting personal protective equipment (PPE) beyond the minimum requirements specified in Article 130.7. This annex focuses on hazard-appropriate layering strategies, environmental conditions, and task-specific adaptations.

## PPE category determination

Incident energy determines the minimum Arc Rating (cal/cm²) required:

- **Category 0**: < 1.2 cal/cm² — Ordinary clothing may suffice
- **Category 1**: 1.2 – 4 cal/cm² — Light-duty FR coveralls
- **Category 2**: 4 – 8 cal/cm² — Heavy-duty FR coveralls + arc hood
- **Category 3**: 8 – 25 cal/cm² — Multilayer switching coat + pants + gloves
- **Category 4**: 25 – 40 cal/cm² — Specialized full-body arc suit (inherent or treated)
- **Category 5**: ≥ 40 cal/cm² — Aluminized proximity suit (only for trained specialists under strict protocols)

Reference: NFPA 70E §130.7(C)(15)(a) – (c) and Table 130.7(C)(15)(a).

## Layering principles for arc-rated clothing

### Inherent versus treated fabrics

**Inherent FR:**
- Aramids (Nomex®, Kevlar®), modacrylics, wool blends
- Do not lose flame-resistance through washing
- Preferred for permanent workplace PPE

**Treated FR:**
- Cotton, cotton/polyester blends with chemical FR treatment
- Effectiveness decreases with laundering
- Require relabeling and inspection after each wash per manufacturer

### Layered assembly strategy

Effective PPE often uses multiple layers:

1. **Base layer**: Lightweight FR undershirt (moisture-wicking if needed)
2. **Primary layer**: Arc-rated coveralls or shirt/pant combination (rated to minimum task requirement)
3. **Outer layer**: Switching jacket or coat (when working near energized equipment at higher voltages)

The cumulative arc rating of layered clothing is NOT simply additive. Incident energy is dissipated through each layer in sequence; outer layers protect inner layers from direct exposure. When specifying PPE:
- Verify total assembly arc rating by manufacturer test data or third-party verification
- Do not assume stacked ratings exceed individual component ratings

### Common assembly examples

| Task | Category | Example assembly |
|------|----------|------------------|
| Low-risk tasks (≤ 4 cal/cm²) | 1–2 | FR shirt + pants + safety glasses |
| Routine panel work (4–8 cal/cm²) | 2–3 | Arc-rated coveralls + arc hood + leather gloves |
| High-energy AC systems (8–25 cal/cm²) | 3 | Switching jacket + arc-rated pants + arc hood + face shield |
| Specialist roles (25–40 cal/cm²) | 4 | Full-body arc suit + undergarments + helmet + face shield |

## Environmental considerations

### Hot weather / summer work

High ambient temperatures increase heat stress when wearing full PPE. Strategies:

- Select lightweight FR fabrics with breathable weaves (within arc-rating requirement)
- Ensure proper ventilation on job sites
- Schedule higher-energy tasks for cooler parts of the day
- Monitor workers for heat exhaustion; enforce rest breaks
- Use lightweight modular layers rather than heavy full-coverage suits when arc rating permits

### Cold weather / winter work

Cold temperatures increase injury severity from electrical contact and require enhanced dexterity:

- Add insulating layers beneath arc-rated PPE (wool or FR long underwear)
- Use insulated, arc-rated gloves rated for both electrical hazard and temperature
- Ensure heavy outer layers do not compromise visibility or mobility
- Check arc-rated glove grip and dexterity when wet or frozen

## Foot and leg protection

### Arc-rated footwear

- Leather safety shoes with arc-rated soles and heels (rated for incident energy class)
- Some manufacturers provide modular arc-rated foot covers that slip over regular safety shoes
- Verify arc rating on footwear label; ordinary steel-toe boots lack arc resistance
- Ensure ankle coverage when working on live equipment above leg level

### Arc-rated socks

- Long socks covering calf (to knee when possible) rated for incident energy
- Worn under regular pants to prevent direct skin exposure in leg/ankle region
- Particularly important in battery rooms, DC systems, and confined-space work

## Eye and face protection

### Arc-flash specific requirements

Standard safety glasses insufficient for arc exposure:

- **Arc-rated face shield**: 4+ mm polycarbonate (minimum) with comfort bridge for nose
- **Arc hood**: Integral with or attached to hood, covering forehead, eyes, cheeks
- Minimum arc rating equal or exceed task incident energy
- Verify ventilation to prevent fogging in humid conditions

### Inserts and combinations

- Some arc hoods accept snap-on magnified lens inserts (for detail work on small components)
- Prescription lens inserts available; verify arc rating of full assembly with prescription inserted
- Goggles with indirect ventilation acceptable under arc hood if face shield primary protection

### Helmet and headwear

- Class C or D hard hats acceptable under arc hoods
- Some integrated hard-hat/arc-hood systems available
- Ensure hard hat retaining chin strap does not interfere with arc hood seal

## Hand protection — leather and rubber

### Rubber insulating gloves

Provide electrical isolation and arc resistance:

- ASTM D120 Class rating denotes voltage class (Class 00 = 500V, Class 0 = 1 kV, up to Class 4 = 36 kV)
- Arc rating not always marked separately; refer to manufacturer data for incident energy resistance
- Always use leather outer gloves over rubber to protect rubber surface and improve grip
- Inspect before each use for tears, punctures, or degradation
- Shelf life typically 5 years from manufacture; monitor expiration dates

### Leather outer gloves

- Protect rubber gloves and provide dexterity
- Arc-rated leather gloves provide additional incident-energy protection
- Select leather weight and construction appropriate to task (lightweight for detail work, heavy for switching)
- Ensure leather gloves rated for the arc incident energy, not solely electrical isolation

### Combination approach

Best practice for live work:
1. Rubber insulating glove (electrical isolation + some arc resistance)
2. Arc-rated leather outer glove (arc resistance + dexterity + protection of rubber glove)

Verify both layers carry appropriate ratings for task voltage and incident energy.

## Confined-space and special adaptations

### Battery rooms and enclosed systems

When working in battery rooms or tightly enclosed spaces:

- Heavy PPE may increase heat stress due to reduced air circulation
- Consider staged entry and work rotation to minimize exposure duration
- Ensure escape routes are clear (PPE must not impede emergency egress)
- Verify arc hood ventilation sufficient to prevent CO₂ buildup in confined air

### Work at height

Full-body arc suit may interfere with fall protection harness. Coordination required:

- Ensure arc suit and harness compatible (straps do not chafe or create pressure points)
- Verify arc rating of harness attachment points and webbing
- Some specialists use modular arc-protection systems that integrate with climbing or access harnesses

### Overhead or inverted work

Arc rating required for head, face, torso, arms, and legs. Additional considerations:

- Hood must not slip or shift during inverted work
- Glove fit critical to maintain dexterity
- Ensure any added bulk does not compromise balance or safety tie-off

## PPE maintenance and care

### Inspection

Inspect all arc-rated PPE before each use:
- Check for tears, burn marks, or chemical stains
- Verify arc hood and face shield optical clarity (no large scratches or cracks)
- Check glove cuffs for cracks in rubber; leather for delamination
- Retire any item showing signs of prior arc exposure

### Washing and maintenance

- **Inherent FR**: Can be laundered repeatedly per standard washing instructions
- **Treated FR**: Degradation occurs with each wash; track wash count and retire per manufacturer specification
- Use mild detergent; avoid bleach or harsh chemicals
- Air-dry when possible; high heat can damage some fabrics

### Storage

- Store PPE in clean, dry location away from direct sunlight
- Rubber gloves: keep away from oils, solvents, and extreme heat
- Folded storage acceptable for coveralls; hang up heavier jackets and suits
- Keep silica-gel packets in sealed containers to manage humidity in humid climates

## Incident energy thresholds and PPE selection

The threshold energy values in NFPA 70E Table 130.7(C)(15)(a) serve as the minimum basis for PPE category assignment. Where site-specific incident-energy calculations are available (e.g., from arc-flash study per IEEE 1584 or NFPA 70E Annex D), use the actual calculated energy to select the appropriate category.

If actual incident energy is calculated lower than the table default, lower PPE categories may be justified. Conversely, conservative estimates or high uncertainty warrant selection of a higher category.

**Reference:** NFPA 70E:2024 §130.7(C)(15) and Annex H.
